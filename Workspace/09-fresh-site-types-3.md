# Fresh Website Types Brainstorm

What websites would people pay $500-$5,000+ to have built — beyond the ones we already know about (architecture portfolios, weddings, vacation rentals, real estate listings, therapist practices, consultant sites, restaurant sites)?

The constraint: static or simple-data-backed sites, no e-commerce/carts/accounts, delivered same-day or next-day, full concierge.

---

## The Full List: 50 Website Types

### Professional Services & Practices

**1. Veterinary clinic / animal hospital site**
- **What:** Practice site with services, team bios, emergency info, new patient forms
- **Who pays:** Vet practice owners, especially new clinics opening
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Credibility for a business where trust matters enormously (people's pets). New clinics need to be live before doors open. One new regular client is worth $2,000+/year in visits.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Same bones every time — team, services, location, hours, emergency protocols.

**2. Dental practice site**
- **What:** Services, team, insurance accepted, new patient info, before/after gallery for cosmetic dentistry
- **Who pays:** Dentists, especially cosmetic/aesthetic practices and new offices
- **Why $500-$5,000:** A cosmetic dentistry case is worth $5,000-$50,000. The site pays for itself with one patient. They compete locally and need to look premium.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Highly templatable shape.

**3. Law firm / solo attorney site**
- **What:** Practice areas, attorney bios, case results, contact/intake
- **Who pays:** Solo practitioners and small firms, especially new practices
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Lawyers bill $200-$800/hr. One new client from the site covers the cost many times over. They need to look established even when they're new.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Same structure across practice areas — family law, personal injury, estate planning, immigration, etc.

**4. Private medical practice / concierge medicine site**
- **What:** Physician bio, approach/philosophy, services, membership details, patient intake
- **Who pays:** Concierge/DPC doctors launching practices
- **Why $500-$5,000:** These doctors charge $2,000-$10,000/year per patient. The site IS their sales channel. They need it to convey a premium, personal experience.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Consistent structure with strong philosophy/approach section.

**5. Accounting / CPA / bookkeeping firm site**
- **What:** Services, team, industry specialties, client portal link, resources
- **Who pays:** Small CPA firms, new practices, bookkeepers going independent
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Tax season creates urgency. One business client is worth $3,000-$20,000/year. They need credibility fast.
- **Repeatable:** Very.

**6. Financial advisor / wealth management site**
- **What:** Advisor bio, investment philosophy, services, credentials, compliance-friendly content
- **Who pays:** Independent RIAs and advisors leaving wirehouses to go independent
- **Why $500-$5,000:** AUM-based advisors managing $10M+ need to look trustworthy and established. One new client relationship can be worth $10,000+/year in fees. Compliance requirements make DIY risky — they want someone who gets it right.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Compliance creates a consistent structure.

### Creative & Portfolio-Driven

**7. Photographer portfolio site**

- **Who pays:** Professional photographers — wedding, portrait, commercial, fine art
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Their portfolio IS their business. A beautiful presentation directly drives bookings. Wedding photographers charge $3,000-$10,000 per wedding, so one booking pays for the site.

**8. Interior designer portfolio site**

- **Who pays:** Interior designers, especially those transitioning from working at firms to going independent
- **Why $500-$5,000:** High-end residential projects are $50K-$500K. The portfolio site is how clients evaluate taste before reaching out. Designers are taste-sensitive and will pay for something that matches their aesthetic.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Project gallery structure with rich photography.

**9. Artist portfolio / exhibition site**
- **What:** Body of work galleries, artist statement, exhibition history, press, representation info
- **Who pays:** Working artists with gallery representation or selling directly
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Artists are notoriously underserved by templates — they need the site to feel like an extension of their work, not a generic frame around it. Gallery relationships and collector trust depend on professional presentation.
- **Repeatable:** Mostly. The structure is consistent but each needs strong aesthetic customization.

**10. Musician / band site**
- **What:** Bio, discography, tour dates, press kit, embedded audio/video, booking contact
- **Who pays:** Working musicians, bands with management, artists releasing albums
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Booking agents and venues check the site. Press needs a press kit. The site is a professional credential. Bands with management have budget for this.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Same sections every time.

**11. Filmmaker / video production company site**
- **What:** Reel, project showcase with embedded video, services, client list, contact
- **Who pays:** Independent filmmakers, production companies, videographers
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Video projects run $5,000-$100,000+. The reel/portfolio site IS the sales pitch. One new client covers the cost.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Video-first layout with project cards.

- **Why $500-$5,000:** A retreat charging $2,000-$5,000/person needs to sell 10-30 spots. The site needs to convey the experience and build trust. One additional registration covers the website cost.
- **Repeatable:** Very. Same shape every time.

**14. Fundraiser / gala / charity event site**

- **Who pays:** Nonprofits, foundations, event committees
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Galas raise $50K-$500K+. The site is the primary marketing vehicle. It needs to look premium because donors and sponsors are evaluating the organization's professionalism.

**15. Memorial / tribute site**

**16. Book launch / author site**

- **Who pays:** Authors (traditionally published with marketing budget, or self-published with resources), publishers for mid-list authors
- **Why $500-$5,000:** Publication dates are fixed deadlines. The site is needed for press, events, and social media. Publishers sometimes provide budget; self-published authors invest from book revenue. A book selling 5,000 copies at $15 makes this trivial.

## The Top 10 Most Interesting Opportunities

These are ranked by the combination of: market size, clear willingness to pay, repeatability, non-obviousness, and strategic fit with same-day AI-built concierge sites.

### 1. Funeral Home Sites
**Why this is the #1 pick:** 19,000 funeral homes in the US alone. Death-adjacent businesses have recession-proof, reliable revenue. The current state of funeral home websites is *awful* — dark, outdated, depressing. A modern, dignified, warm site is a massive upgrade. Funeral directors are busy professionals who want someone to just handle it. The structure is nearly identical every time (services, staff, current obituaries, pre-planning, grief resources). You could build a vertical practice around this alone. And here's the kicker: funeral homes also need individual memorial/tribute pages for each death — which means ongoing per-event revenue from the same client relationship. $2,000-$3,000 for the main site, $500-$1,000 per memorial page, ongoing.

### 2. Event Venue Sites
**Why this is fascinating:** Every barn, estate, rooftop, gallery, and historic building that hosts events needs a site that sells the *experience* of the space. The photography is already done (venues invest in professional photos). Venue rental fees are $3,000-$25,000+, so the ROI is immediate. Couples browse dozens of venue sites when planning weddings — this is high-volume, high-scrutiny browsing. The AI chat feature could be killer here: "Does this venue have outdoor space for 150?" "Is there a rain plan?" "What's the noise curfew?" — questions planners ask every time. Venues are also a referral network into wedding sites.

### 3. Contractor / Home Services Sites
**Why this is enormous:** There are millions of licensed contractors in the US (general contractors, roofers, electricians, plumbers, landscapers, HVAC, pool builders, painters). Most have terrible sites or no site. Homeowners Google "roofer near me" and judge entirely by the website. The before/after project gallery is the killer feature, and the structure is identical every time. One roofing job ($8,000-$15,000) pays for the site many times over. This is a massive volume market with clear willingness to pay.

### 4. Conference / Summit / Symposium Sites
**Why this is great:** Hard deadlines create urgency (the event date is fixed). Events have real budgets with a marketing/website line item. The structure is perfectly repeatable (speakers, schedule, venue, registration). Speed is enormously valuable — event organizers often need the site live weeks or days before the event. There are thousands of professional conferences, industry summits, and corporate events every year. And each event is a one-time site that needs to be rebuilt annually — recurring revenue.

### 5. Political Candidate Sites (Local/State)
**Why this is a sleeper:** Thousands of local and state races every election cycle — city council, state legislature, school board, judges, sheriff, county commissioner. Candidates announce and need a site *immediately*. Campaign budgets are real (even local races raise $10K-$100K+). The structure is perfectly repeatable (bio, platform, endorsements, donate, volunteer). Speed is the entire value — campaigns move fast. Every two years, a fresh wave of demand. This is seasonal but predictable and high-volume.

### 6. Summer Camp Sites
**Why this is underrated:** Camp enrollment is competitive and seasonal with hard deadlines. Parents research camps extensively online. Camp sessions cost $500-$10,000+ per child, so the ROI per enrollment is clear. Many camp sites are shockingly bad — built in 2008 and never updated. There are ~14,000 day and resident camps in the US. Camp directors are busy people who want someone to handle it. The AI chat feature could answer the #1 parent question: "Is this the right camp for my kid?"

### 7. Public Speaker / Thought Leader Sites
**Why this is high-value:** Speaking fees are $5,000-$50,000+ per engagement. The site is literally how speakers get booked — event organizers browse speaker sites to evaluate topic fit, speaking style (video), and credentials. One additional booking pays for the site 10x over. The structure is perfectly repeatable. Many speakers have terrible sites or just use a speaker bureau listing. The press kit / media kit section is a natural fit for what you build.

### 8. Pet Breeder Sites
**Why this is non-obvious gold:** Responsible breeders sell puppies for $2,000-$5,000+. Buyers research breeders extensively — health testing, lineage, facility, philosophy. A professional site is the #1 signal that distinguishes a responsible breeder from a backyard operation. The structure is highly repeatable (parent dogs, health testing, available puppies, application process). The emotional stakes are high (people are choosing a family member). Many breeders' sites are terrible WordPress disasters. And there's a natural repeat cycle — as new litters arrive, the site updates.

### 9. Doula / Midwife / Birth Worker Sites
**Why this fits perfectly:** Birth work is philosophy-heavy — clients choose based on alignment with the provider's approach. This is exactly where your "carrying forward the implying" superpower shines. A doula who believes in physiological birth has a completely different site than one who supports all birth choices. The site needs to convey trust, warmth, and competence. Doula packages are $1,500-$4,000, so one new client covers the cost. This is a growing market as more families seek birth support.

### 10. B&B / Boutique Inn Sites
**Why this is strategic:** Boutique inns compete on character and story — exactly what templates fail at. A B&B in a converted Victorian mansion needs a site that conveys the *feeling* of staying there, not a Marriott-style room listing. Direct bookings avoid 15-20% OTA commission, so driving even a few bookings through the site saves thousands per year. The AI chat feature is perfect: "Is there a room with a fireplace?" "Can we bring our dog?" "What restaurants are walking distance?" The site structure (rooms, story, area, booking) is perfectly repeatable.

## Patterns Worth Noting

**The "trust-dependent local service" pattern:** Funeral homes, contractors, veterinarians, dentists, auto mechanics, moving companies — businesses where the customer is entrusting something valuable (their body, their pet, their home, their belongings, their dead) and evaluates trust primarily through web presence. These businesses have high lifetime customer value, terrible existing sites, and no time/desire to manage web stuff. This could be an entire vertical strategy.

**The "event with a deadline" pattern:** Conferences, galas, political campaigns, retreats, summer camps — anything with a fixed date creates urgency that makes speed enormously valuable. These are also naturally recurring (annual events, election cycles, camp seasons), creating predictable repeat demand.

**The "portfolio drives revenue" pattern:** Photographers, videographers, tattoo artists, jewelry designers, speakers — anyone whose work samples directly drive purchasing decisions. The quality of the portfolio presentation directly correlates to income. These people know what good looks like and will pay for it.

**The "gateway + ongoing" pattern:** Funeral homes (main site + individual memorials), event venues (site + per-event pages), breeders (site + litter updates), camps (site + annual refresh) — clients where the initial site naturally leads to recurring per-event work.

**The unsexy-but-enormous pattern:** Most of the biggest opportunities by volume are not glamorous — funeral homes, contractors, dentists, moving companies. Nobody brags about building funeral home websites. But the market size is massive, the current state of these sites is terrible, the willingness to pay is clear, and the shape is perfectly repeatable. Glamour is not the goal; volume and reliability are.
