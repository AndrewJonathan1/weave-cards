### 1. Independent Consultant / Fractional Executive Practice Launch

**What the complete process is:**

When a senior professional leaves corporate to hang a shingle — as a fractional CFO, CMO, COO, strategy consultant, or specialized advisor — they face a sudden wall of 15-20 distinct deliverables they've never had to create:

- Positioning statement and professional narrative ("What do I actually do?")
- Service tier/package definitions with pricing
- Proposal template (that doesn't look like a corporate deck)
- Master services agreement / contract template
- Statement of work template
- Invoice template and payment terms
- Client onboarding questionnaire
- Project kickoff framework
- Status update / reporting template
- Pipeline / CRM tracking system (spreadsheet-based)
- Outreach email sequences (warm network, cold, referral ask)
- LinkedIn profile rewrite and content strategy outline
- Case study / engagement summary framework
- Rate card / fee schedule
- Referral partner communication templates
- Quarterly business review template (for retainer clients)
- Off-boarding / project wrap checklist

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

Google Docs for contracts (found a template online, vaguely edited it). Canva for a logo they don't love. A proposal they wrote in a panic at midnight before their first prospect call, which they keep reusing even though it doesn't really fit. A spreadsheet they made once to track leads but abandoned. LinkedIn posts they draft but never publish because the tone feels off. They keep meaning to "systematize" but every day is client work.

**Why coherence matters here:**

A consultant's professional presence IS their product. When the proposal, contract, onboarding, and status reporting all feel like they were made by the same person with the same level of care and the same professional voice, the client feels they are working with a real firm — not a person winging it. Incoherence signals amateur. Coherence signals "I've done this a hundred times."

**Where the money comes from:**

These consultants charge $150-500/hour. Their average engagement is $10K-100K+. They would gladly pay $2,000-5,000 to have their entire practice infrastructure built coherently, because one additional client won pays for it 10x over. The ROI is immediately obvious to them.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

These are often brilliant operators who have spent 20 years *inside* organizations. They know exactly what great consulting delivery looks like from the *client* side. But they have never had to package themselves. They feel a strong sense of what their practice should feel like — authoritative but approachable, specialized but flexible — but they cannot write it down. Every template they find online feels generic, corporate, or wrong. They need someone to ask the right questions and then translate their implicit expertise into explicit materials.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A single 60-90 minute conversation where you understand: their background, their ideal client, their superpower, their working style, their fee preferences, their comfort with sales. From that conversation, you produce EVERYTHING — all 17 deliverables listed above — in a coherent system with consistent voice, formatting, and professional logic. Delivered as an organized Google Drive folder or Notion workspace. The consultant opens it and thinks "this is what I would have built if I had 200 hours and a design team."

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

The people building SaaS tools for consultants (HoneyBook, Dubsado, Practice) build software platforms — they make you learn their system and fill in fields. They give you a blank canvas with features. What the consultant needs is not a platform but a *populated* system built specifically for them. The tech people think the answer is "build an app." The consultants think the answer is "hire a VA or a branding agency for $15K." Nobody has connected "deep conversation + AI generation + coherent system delivery."

### 2. Therapist / Clinician Private Practice Setup

**What the complete process is:**

A licensed therapist, psychologist, social worker, speech-language pathologist, or counselor leaving a group practice or agency to start their own:

- Practice philosophy / about page copy
- Informed consent document
- Practice policies (cancellation, payment, communication boundaries)
- Intake paperwork (adult, adolescent, couples — depending on specialty)
- Assessment/screening tool selection and implementation
- Treatment plan template
- Progress note template (compliant with their licensing board)
- Superbill template for insurance reimbursement
- Good Faith Estimate (No Surprises Act compliance)
- Sliding scale policy and application
- Crisis safety plan template
- Referral list / resource guide for clients
- Professional referral outreach templates
- Client communication templates (appointment reminders, between-session check-ins)
- Group therapy curriculum outline (if applicable)
- Workshop/psychoeducation materials
- Psychology Today profile optimization
- Google Business Profile setup content
- Telehealth consent and procedures
- HIPAA notice of privacy practices

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

They buy a template pack from a therapist-influencer on Etsy for $97 — it's generic, doesn't match their modality or population, and they spend weeks editing it. They copy intake forms from their old practice (which may not be legally theirs to use). They find a consent form template online that doesn't match their state's requirements. They write their Psychology Today profile late at night and it reads like a textbook. They use SimplePractice or TherapyNotes for scheduling but still have disconnected documents everywhere. Nothing sounds like *them*.

**Why coherence matters here:**

A client's first experience of a therapist is their paperwork. If the intake form feels warm and thoughtful, if the consent document is clear but not coldly legal, if the policies feel boundaried but compassionate — the therapeutic relationship begins before the first session. Incoherent paperwork (some formal, some casual, some clearly copied from somewhere else) unconsciously signals "this person doesn't have their act together" — the opposite of what a therapy client needs to feel.

**Where the money comes from:**

Therapists in private practice earn $80-250/session, seeing 15-25 clients/week. A full practice generates $100K-300K/year. They would pay $1,500-3,500 for a complete, coherent practice document system because: (a) the alternative is spending 40-80 hours doing it themselves (time they could be seeing clients), and (b) getting it wrong has compliance risks. The market is enormous — there are 600,000+ licensed therapists in the US, and the trend toward private practice has accelerated massively post-COVID.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Therapists are masters of understanding other people's internal experiences but often struggle to articulate their own professional identity in writing. They *know* what their practice should feel like — warm, safe, specific to their population — but writing it down feels excruciating. They're trained in listening, not marketing. The gap between "I know I'm great with anxious high-achievers" and "here's a Psychology Today profile that makes anxious high-achievers feel seen" is enormous.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation that asks: What's your theoretical orientation? What populations do you work with? What's your clinical style — warm and informal, or more structured? What are your state's specific requirements? What modalities do you use? What does your ideal client need to feel in their first interaction with you? From this, you generate the full document system — intake forms that reflect their modality (a DBT-oriented therapist gets different intake questions than a psychodynamic one), a consent document that sounds like a human wrote it, policies that balance clinical boundaries with genuine warmth, and a Psychology Today profile that actually sounds like them.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

EHR platforms (SimplePractice, TherapyNotes) provide blank templates but don't customize them. Therapy consultants charge $3,000-10,000 for practice setup but work slowly and mainly coach, not produce. Etsy template sellers provide generic documents. The clinical specificity required (different orientations, different populations, different state laws) has made it seem like you'd need a clinician to customize each one — but an AI that asks the right questions can handle this variation.

### 3. Nonprofit Startup / Organization Launch

**What the complete process is:**

Someone passionate about a cause — a community leader, a retired professional, a social entrepreneur — starting a formal nonprofit:

- Mission, vision, and values statements
- Articles of incorporation (state-specific)
- Bylaws
- Conflict of interest policy
- Board governance documents (roles, term limits, meeting procedures)
- IRS Form 1023 or 1023-EZ narrative sections
- Organizational budget template
- Fundraising strategy and annual plan
- Donor acknowledgment letter templates
- Grant prospect research framework
- Grant application narrative (boilerplate sections)
- Program description documents
- Theory of change / logic model
- Impact measurement framework
- Volunteer role descriptions and onboarding materials
- Board member recruitment packet
- Annual report template
- Donor/stakeholder newsletter template
- Event planning frameworks
- Partnership outreach templates

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

Free templates from Foundation Group or state nonprofit association websites (legalese they don't understand). A fiscal sponsor they're paying 5-10% to while they figure out 501(c)(3). Board members who "know someone who did this" sharing conflicting advice. A consultant they can barely afford who charges $5,000-15,000 to handle just the IRS application. A WordPress site they started but abandoned. Grant applications where they rewrite their mission description slightly differently each time because they never locked it down.

**Why coherence matters here:**

A nonprofit's credibility is its currency. When a funder reads the grant application, then visits the website, then sees the annual report — and all three tell the same clear, compelling story with the same language — the organization reads as legitimate, well-managed, and worthy of investment. Incoherence between documents is a red flag for funders. New nonprofits lose grants not because their work isn't good, but because they can't present coherently.

**Where the money comes from:**

Two revenue sources: (a) the nonprofit founder pays $2,000-5,000 for the complete setup (compared to $5K-15K for a nonprofit startup consultant who does less), and (b) the downstream impact — a coherent, fundable nonprofit captures grants and donations 2-5x faster than a scrambling one. Founders know this and feel the urgency. There are ~80,000 new nonprofits formed per year in the US.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Nonprofit founders are typically mission-driven people with deep community knowledge and passion. They are not bureaucrats, lawyers, or grant writers. They feel their mission intensely but translating that feeling into the precise, strategic language that funders expect — specific, measurable, theory-of-change-aligned — is like speaking a foreign language. They know what the world needs. They cannot articulate it the way the institutional world demands.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation that deeply explores: What change do you want to see? Who are you serving? What do you actually do (programs, activities)? What's your story — why you? How will you know it's working? Who will fund this? From this, generate everything: incorporation documents, bylaws, the IRS narrative, a grant boilerplate, a fundraising plan, board documents, donor communications, and program descriptions — all telling one unified, compelling story. The founder opens the folder and thinks "this is exactly what was in my head, but I could never get it out."

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

Nonprofit startup services are dominated by lawyers (who handle legal formation but not strategy or communications) and consultants (who are expensive and slow). Software like Guidestar, Bloomerang, or Little Green Light handles donor management but not organizational document creation. The knowledge required spans legal, strategic, communications, and programmatic — a rare combination that AI can actually bridge when guided by the right conversation.

### 4. Real Estate Agent Listing & Client System

**What the complete process is:**

A real estate agent — especially one going from a team to solo, or leveling up from part-time to full-time — needs a complete operational system:

- Listing presentation (pre-listing pitch deck)
- Comparative Market Analysis template framework
- Seller onboarding package (timeline, expectations, preparation checklist)
- Home staging guide (general + price-point specific)
- Property photography shot list and preparation checklist
- Property description writing framework (MLS, Zillow, marketing)
- Marketing flyer / just-listed templates
- Open house sign-in and follow-up system
- Buyer consultation presentation
- Buyer needs assessment questionnaire
- Offer preparation checklist
- Transaction coordination checklists (buyer side, seller side)
- Closing gift and follow-up sequence
- Client communication templates (status updates, market updates, anniversary check-ins)
- Sphere of influence nurture sequences (past clients, professional network)
- Referral request templates
- Social media content frameworks (just listed, just sold, market update, neighborhood spotlight)
- Neighborhood/community guide templates
- Relocation guide for out-of-area buyers
- Open house planning and marketing system

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

Their brokerage provides some templates but they're generic and brand-locked. Canva for marketing materials (inconsistent design, hours spent). A CRM (Follow Up Boss, kvCORE) they set up once and barely use because they didn't configure the sequences. Scripts they learned in coaching that feel robotic. A listing presentation they saw a top producer use and tried to copy. Property descriptions they agonize over for an hour each because they're not writers.

**Why coherence matters here:**

In real estate, you ARE the brand. When a potential seller receives a listing presentation, sees your Instagram, gets your market update email, and then sees your open house materials — and everything looks, sounds, and feels like the same professional — they think: "This person has a system. This person is a professional. I trust this person with the largest transaction of my life." Fragmented, inconsistent materials signal a part-timer.

**Where the money comes from:**

Average commission per transaction: $8,000-15,000+. Agents who present professionally win more listings. One additional listing per year pays for this service many times over. Agents spend $2,000-10,000/year on coaching, $500-2,000/year on tech tools — spending $2,000-4,000 for a complete operational system is well within their budget and immediately productive. There are ~1.5 million active real estate agents in the US.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Most agents are relationship people, not systems people or writers. They know intuitively what makes a great client experience — they've seen top producers do it — but they cannot reverse-engineer the system behind it. They know their listing presentation should be "compelling but not salesy" and their follow-up should be "persistent but not annoying" but translating those feelings into actual documents and sequences is paralyzing. They buy coaching and templates but still can't make it *theirs*.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation that understands: What's your market (luxury, first-time, investment, specific neighborhoods)? What's your personality and communication style? What makes you different from the 50 other agents in your zip code? What's your ideal client? What does your best client experience look like from first contact to closing? From this, build the entire operational system — every template, every sequence, every guide — in their voice, for their market, telling their story consistently. Delivered in a ready-to-use format.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

Real estate coaching programs ($500-1,000/month) teach concepts but don't build your materials. Real estate tech companies build CRMs and marketing platforms (blank canvases). Template sellers on Etsy sell generic one-offs. Nobody sits down and builds the whole system, customized, coherent, in one shot — because before AI, that would take a human team weeks.

### 5. Airbnb / Short-Term Rental Host Operations

**What the complete process is:**

A property owner turning one or more properties into short-term rentals:

- Listing descriptions (platform-optimized for Airbnb, VRBO, Booking.com)
- Property photography shot list and staging guide
- House rules document
- Guest welcome guide / digital guidebook (arrival instructions, WiFi, appliances, house quirks)
- Local area guide (restaurants, activities, emergency services, grocery stores)
- Check-in/check-out procedure documentation
- Guest communication sequences (booking confirmation, pre-arrival, during stay, checkout, review request)
- Cleaning checklist (turnover protocol)
- Cleaning team coordination procedures
- Maintenance issue response templates
- Damage/security deposit claim templates
- Guest complaint response templates
- Review response templates (positive and negative)
- Co-host agreement (if applicable)
- Pricing strategy framework (seasonal, event-based)
- Neighbor communication templates
- Emergency procedures document
- Inventory and supplies restocking checklist
- Property inspection checklist
- Tax documentation and tracking framework

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

They write their listing description at 11pm and it reads like it. They have check-in instructions in a Notes app they text to guests (different every time). Their cleaning checklist is verbal. They respond to guest messages reactively, reinventing each reply. They have a "house rules" printed sheet from 2019. Their local recommendations are whatever they remember in the moment. They use Hospitable or Guesty for channel management but the templates inside are blank or generic.

**Why coherence matters here:**

Guest experience is cumulative. When the listing description sets a tone ("A calm retreat in wine country"), and the welcome guide maintains it, and the local recommendations reinforce it, and the communication feels warm and consistent — the guest leaves a 5-star review. When the listing says "luxury" but the house rules sound aggressive, the local guide is a random bullet list, and the check-in instructions are confusing — the guest feels a disconnect and reviews reflect it. Coherence directly drives the rating that determines income.

**Where the money comes from:**

Average STR revenue: $30,000-80,000+ per property per year. The difference between a 4.5 and 4.8 rating is roughly 15-25% in bookings. Hosts would pay $1,500-3,000 per property for a complete, coherent operational system. The market is massive: 1.5 million+ STR listings in the US. Multi-property hosts would pay per property. Hosts also spend heavily on optimization already ($200-500/month on tools, plus cleaners, photographers, etc.).

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Hosts often have great taste and a clear vision for the *experience* they want to create — "relaxing mountain cabin," "stylish urban loft," "family beach house" — but they are not writers or hospitality professionals. They cannot translate "cozy but modern" into listing copy that performs well on Airbnb's algorithm. They know their property is special but can't articulate *why* in a way that books. They know what great hospitality operations look like when they experience it at a hotel, but they can't reverse-engineer that system for their property.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation exploring: Tell me about your property — the vibe, the location, what people love about it. Who's your ideal guest? What's the neighborhood like? What's your hosting style — hands-on or hands-off? What are your non-negotiables? What problems have you had with guests? From this, produce the entire operational system: listing descriptions that perform, a branded digital guidebook, communication sequences that build warmth, a cleaning protocol that ensures consistency, review responses that protect the brand, house rules that set boundaries without sounding hostile — all in one voice.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

STR tech (Guesty, Hospitable, OwnerRez) provides infrastructure but not content. Hospitality consultants charge $2,000-5,000 and work slowly. Etsy template sellers offer guidebook templates but they're generic. The STR world is dominated by tech-forward optimization tools (pricing algorithms, channel managers) — nobody has focused on the *content and communication layer* that actually drives guest experience and reviews.

Quarterly reviews are a different format every time. Their "onboarding process" is whatever they remember to do. Client newsletters are sporadic, inconsistent, or outsourced to a generic content service that sounds nothing like them. Their discovery meeting is "winging it with experience."

**Why coherence matters here:**

People hand their life savings to their financial advisor. Trust is everything. When every touchpoint — the discovery meeting, the plan presentation, the quarterly review, the market commentary — feels like it comes from the same thoughtful, organized, professional mind, the client feels safe. Incoherence breeds anxiety. A client who receives a polished plan but then a sloppy quarterly review starts wondering about attention to detail — with their money.

**Where the money comes from:**

Independent advisors manage $50M-500M+ in assets, earning 0.5-1% AUM. A practice managing $100M earns $500K-1M/year. They routinely invest $3,000-10,000 in practice management, coaching, and tools. A complete, coherent client system for $3,000-5,000 is a clear value proposition, especially if it helps them win one additional $1M+ client. There are ~300,000 financial advisors in the US, with a strong trend toward independence.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Financial advisors are often great with people and with numbers, but many struggle with written communication and system design. They know what "white glove service" means — they can describe the feeling — but they can't produce the documents that create that experience consistently. They know their quarterly reviews should make clients feel informed and reassured, but they spend 3 hours per client cobbling together performance data with narrative and it still doesn't feel right. They want to feel like a boutique firm, not a one-person shop.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation that explores: What's your investment philosophy? Who's your ideal client (pre-retirees, business owners, tech employees with equity comp)? What's your communication style? What do your best clients say about working with you? What does your service model look like? From this, build every client-facing document, meeting framework, and communication template — all reflecting one consistent investment philosophy, one consistent voice, one consistent level of care.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

Compliance complexity scares away most tech companies. Practice management coaches (like CEG Worldwide) charge $10K-30K/year and focus on strategy, not document production. CRM platforms (Redtail, Wealthbox) provide infrastructure but not content. The compliance overlay makes people think this requires expensive human professionals — but the documents themselves are well-structured and AI-generatable, with compliance review as a final human step.

### 7. Author Book Launch System

**What the complete process is:**

An author — self-published or traditionally published — preparing to launch a book:

- Media kit / press kit (author bio in multiple lengths, professional headshot specs, book description in multiple lengths, endorsement highlights, speaking topics)
- Press release
- Book description / jacket copy (Amazon-optimized, Goodreads, publisher catalog)
- Email to personal network announcing the book
- Launch team recruitment materials (what to ask of early readers)
- Advance Reader Copy (ARC) distribution strategy and communication
- Amazon author page and category strategy
- Book launch timeline (12-week, 8-week, 4-week countdowns)
- Social media content calendar and post templates
- Email newsletter sequence (pre-launch, launch week, post-launch)
- Podcast/media interview pitch templates (customizable for different shows)
- Speaking engagement pitch
- Book club discussion guide
- One-sheet / sell sheet for bookstore buyers
- Event planning frameworks (launch party, readings, signings)
- Review solicitation strategies and templates
- Endorsement request templates
- Cross-promotion and author collaboration outreach
- Post-launch sustain plan

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

Their publisher's marketing department gives them a template press kit but expects them to fill it in. They write their own Amazon description and it reads like a college essay. They cobble together social media posts one at a time, each with a different tone. They send a rambling email to friends saying "buy my book." Their podcast pitch is a copy-paste of the book description — which wasn't written to pitch, it was written to describe. They Google "how to launch a book" and find 40 contradictory blog posts. They hire a book publicist for $3,000-10,000 who sends out press releases that go nowhere.

**Why coherence matters here:**

A book launch has a narrow window — the first 2-4 weeks determine Amazon ranking, which determines discoverability, which determines the book's entire commercial trajectory. Every touchpoint needs to tell the same compelling story: the Amazon page, the podcast interview, the Instagram post, the email to a book club. When the message is coherent, momentum builds. When it's fragmented, each effort exists in isolation and energy dissipates.

**Where the money comes from:**

Self-published authors spend $2,000-10,000 on launch (editing, cover, marketing). Traditionally published authors are increasingly expected to fund their own marketing ($1,000-5,000+). A coherent launch system for $2,000-4,000 is competitive with a publicist (who does less) and demonstrably better than DIY (which produces fragment). More importantly, the financial upside of a successful launch — ongoing book sales, speaking fees ($2,000-25,000/engagement), course opportunities — makes this high-ROI. There are ~4 million books published per year, growing fast.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Authors can write 80,000-word books but often cannot write a 150-word book description that sells. The skills are completely different. They are too close to the material. They know what the book *is* but not what to say about it that makes someone want to read it. They know what makes it special but can't compress that into a pitch. They wrote it over two years and the idea of summarizing it in a tweet feels impossible. They need someone to pull the "why should anyone care" out of them.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation that asks: What is this book really about — not the plot, the *why*? Who is it for? What will they feel after reading it? What makes this different from similar books? What's your story — why did you write this? What are you comfortable with in terms of self-promotion? From this, generate the entire launch system — every document, template, sequence, and strategy — all telling one story, all in one voice, all working together.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

Book publicists are expensive and focused on media placement, not system building. Book marketing courses teach concepts but don't produce the materials. Amazon optimization tools handle metadata but not messaging. The publishing industry is notoriously slow to adopt new technology. Authors are typically creative people who resist systematization — but they need it desperately.

When someone dies, the surviving family member (often one person bears the burden) faces an overwhelming cascade of administrative tasks — usually while grieving:

- Obituary (multiple versions: newspaper, online, social media, funeral program)
- Funeral/memorial service planning documents (program, readings, music selections, logistics)
- Death certificate request tracking and distribution list
- Notification list and letter templates (employer, Social Security, VA, insurance companies, banks, credit cards, subscriptions, memberships, utilities)
- Probate filing documents and timeline
- Executor's checklist and timeline
- Estate inventory spreadsheet
- Beneficiary notification letters
- Asset transfer documentation checklist
- Insurance claim filing guides and templates
- Tax filing requirements (final individual return, estate return)
- Digital account inventory and closure/memorialization procedures
- Real property management/disposition planning
- Vehicle title transfer documentation
- Debt notification and settlement letters
- Family communication templates (keeping relatives informed of estate progress)
- Memorial fund or scholarship setup materials (if applicable)
- Charitable donation acknowledgment tracking
- Personal property distribution framework (who gets what)
- Timeline and task tracking system for the entire process

**What tools people currently cobble together:**

They Google "what to do when someone dies" and get a 47-item checklist with no actual documents. They call companies one by one, each requiring different forms and documentation. They write the obituary under time pressure and it doesn't capture the person. They track nothing and forget which accounts they've closed. They get conflicting advice from family members, attorneys, and the internet. They hire an estate attorney for the legal parts ($2,000-10,000) but still have to handle all the administrative work themselves. They buy a book about estate settlement but it's 300 pages and they're grieving.

**Why coherence matters here:**

The person managing this process is overwhelmed, grieving, and typically doing this for the first time. A coherent system — where everything is organized, tracked, and templated — is the difference between drowning and feeling in control. When they can see the full picture of what needs to happen, in what order, with actual documents ready to send, the administrative burden drops by 80%. This is about cognitive load reduction during the worst time of someone's life.

**Where the money comes from:**

Estate settlement affects everyone eventually — 3 million+ deaths per year in the US. The estate itself often has assets that easily justify the cost. Families routinely spend $5,000-20,000 on estate settlement (attorney, accountant, etc.). A complete, personalized administrative system for $1,500-3,000 would be eagerly adopted — especially because the alternative is hundreds of hours of confused, emotional labor. This could also be offered through funeral homes, estate attorneys, or financial advisors as a value-add service (B2B channel).

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

The person managing this has never done it before. They don't know what they don't know. They can't articulate what they need because they don't know the process. They just know they're overwhelmed and things keep arriving in the mail that demand action. They need someone to say "here's everything that needs to happen, here are the documents you need, and here's a system to track it all" — and also to handle the emotional dimension: the obituary should actually sound like the person who died, not like a form.

**What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

A conversation (ideally with someone empathetic) that gathers: Who passed away? What's the family situation? What assets exist (roughly)? Is there a will? Where did they live? What are the immediate needs (funeral planning vs. already handled)? From this, generate a personalized, comprehensive system: the full notification list with pre-drafted letters, the probate timeline, the asset inventory, the obituary options (capturing the person's actual life and personality), the family update templates, the task tracker. Everything organized, everything connected, everything ready to use.

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

It's a sensitive market that tech companies avoid (hard to A/B test death). Estate attorneys handle the legal slice but not the administrative mountain. Funeral homes handle the ceremony but not the aftermath. The process crosses legal, financial, administrative, and emotional domains — no single profession owns it. Companies like Empathy (app) and Lantern have started addressing this but with generic tool-based approaches, not personalized document generation.

**Why the translation gap is especially painful here:**

Microschool founders are typically passionate educators or parents with strong convictions about how children should learn — Montessori-influenced, nature-based, classical, project-based, etc. They feel their philosophy deeply but translating it into a documented, operational system is overwhelming. **What the AI-powered coherent version would look like:**

**Why this hasn't been built yet:**

Traditional education companies sell to districts, not individuals. Homeschool curriculum providers sell curriculum but not operational systems. The microschool movement is too new and too diverse (every school has a different philosophy) for one-size-fits-all solutions. The people starting microschools are educators who distrust corporate ed-tech — but they desperately need operational infrastructure.
